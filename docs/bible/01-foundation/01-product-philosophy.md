# Bible Product Philosophy

## Core belief

People do not need another feed.

People need a simple bridge from intention to real-life action.

GO IRL exists because many people want to do things in real life, but the path from idea to actual attendance is broken by friction:

- too much scrolling;
- unclear plans;
- low trust;
- social fear;
- fragmented chats;
- no clear host;
- no simple join path;
- no reminder that the goal is to show up.

## Mission

Less scrolling. More living.

This mission is not marketing decoration. It is the main product constraint.

If a feature increases time in the app but does not increase real attendance, it is suspicious.

## Product promise

GO IRL helps a person answer quickly:

- What is happening?
- When?
- Where?
- Who is going?
- Is there a spot for me?
- Can I join without awkward negotiation?
- Can I trust this enough to show up?

## Anti-feed principle

GO IRL should not reward passive consumption.

The product should avoid:

- endless content feeds;
- vanity metrics;
- public popularity contests;
- addictive loops;
- algorithmic scrolling;
- social comparison;
- post-event content traps.

The product should reward:

- creating a real plan;
- inviting people;
- joining clearly;
- coordinating briefly;
- attending in real life;
- returning because life improved offline.

## Telegram-first principle

GO IRL starts where people already coordinate: Telegram.

The app should make Telegram coordination better, not replace every social behavior with a separate network.

Telegram-first means:

- fast open from shared link;
- simple `startapp` flow;
- minimal account friction;
- event chat close to the event;
- no unnecessary standalone social network behavior in MVP.

## Local-first principle

The product begins with Olomouc because local density matters more than broad catalog size.

A small city with real usage is more valuable than a large empty map.

Closed beta should prove:

- event creation works;
- sharing creates joins;
- joins create chat activity;
- chat activity increases attendance;
- people are willing to return.

## Trust principle

The hardest part is not listing events.

The hardest part is making a person comfortable enough to attend.

Trust can come from:

- clear event details;
- visible participant count;
- organizer clarity;
- temporary event chat;
- sport coach support;
- beginner-friendly copy;
- no surprise behavior;
- explicit privacy boundaries.

## Small-event principle

GO IRL is optimized for small real-life activities, not massive events.

The MVP should prefer:

- simple capacity;
- clear spots left;
- direct join;
- private join request where needed;
- short coordination window;
- real attendance.

Ticketing, large venues, paid promotion, and public event marketing are future scope.

## Beginner comfort principle

Many users hesitate because they fear arriving alone, not knowing the rules, or feeling out of place.

GO IRL should reduce this fear through:

- event clarity;
- beginner-friendly category copy;
- Sport Coach for sport events;
- future event roles only after Sport Coach validation;
- temporary chat close to event details.

## MVP restraint principle

The product must resist becoming too many products at once.

Before beta, GO IRL is not:

- a dating app;
- a friends app;
- a travel app;
- a ticketing app;
- a club CRM;
- a city calendar;
- a generic social network.

It may later grow into verticals, but only after the real-life loop is proven.

## Product quality principle

A smaller stable MVP is better than a larger confusing product.

MVP 1.1 should focus on:

- clear cards;
- reliable share links;
- browser demo mode;
- stable profile basics;
- safe Supabase boundaries;
- readable event details;
- working chat and coach areas;
- QA gates before beta claims.

## Success definition

GO IRL succeeds when users say:

```text
I saw it, joined it, chatted briefly, and actually went.
```

The product should be judged by real attendance, not by screen time.
